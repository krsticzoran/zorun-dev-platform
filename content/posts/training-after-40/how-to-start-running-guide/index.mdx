Running seems like a perfectly simple activity. When I first started about six years ago, I thought the same: just lace up my shoes, head out the door, and run. However, if the goal is to finish those first 5km without injury, there are rules we need to respect. This is especially true if you are well past 35 and have been inactive for years.

## The Two Most Common Newcomers

Generally, we see two types of beginners hitting the pavement. The first type runs a kilometer or two, gets completely out of breath, and quickly concludes, "This isn't for me." They give up before they’ve even truly started, discouraged that their workout lasted only ten minutes before they "exploded."

The second group consists of more ambitious runners. They head out and run as fast and as far as they can, repeating this intensity session after session. After a week or two, they begin to feel aches in their tendons and joints, leading them to think, "I’m just too old for this."

## The Solution: The Run-Walk Method

The best solution for all beginners, regardless of which group they fall into, is the "run-walk" method. Essentially, this is a technique where, for example, you run for one minute and then walk for one minute. Running is a demanding activity not just for your heart and lungs, but also for your joints and tendons.

By using the run-walk method, we allow our bodies to ease into this new activity without starting too hard and risking a total system collapse. The goal isn't to exhaust ourselves; it's to provide the body with an adequate level of stress that it can actually adapt to.

## 12-Week Run-Walk Progression Plan

The goal of this plan is to safely build your aerobic base. Each week consists of 3 sessions. Always start with a 5-minute brisk walk as a warm-up.

### A Note on Consistency

Don't worry if you need to repeat a specific week. The "35+" rule is simple: listen to your body. If your joints feel stiff or the breathlessness is too intense, stay at the current level for another week before progressing.

If you have started this program and feel it is too easy, I still advise you not to skip any steps. This especially applies to everyone over 35 who hasn't been physically active in the last few years or is carrying a few extra pounds.

In running, patience is the key to success. You must not overstimulate the body, which is even more critical as we age because the body requires more time for recovery and adaptation. With age, the recovery process from every session becomes longer; therefore, the primary goal for recreational runners in their later years should be to minimize injuries throughout their journey. 

## The Golden Rule: Talk While You Run

One of the biggest mistakes beginners make is running too fast. You should be able to maintain a conversation while running. If you find yourself gasping for air, slow down even if it feels like you're barely moving faster than a walk. Your heart and lungs need to build a base before your legs can go fast.

## Conclusion: Less is More

Let your running motto be: **"Less is more."** It is better to finish a workout feeling like you could have done a little more than to finish it feeling like you’ll never want to run again. 

Consistency beats intensity every single time. See you on the track!
