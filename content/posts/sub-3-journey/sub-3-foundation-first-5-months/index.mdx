Running has always been my passion; it was only a matter of time before I laced up my shoes again. For the past three years, I poured everything into my career, but that success came at a price. I created an imbalance that finally started to affect my health. High blood pressure was my wake-up call—a loud reminder that a sedentary life at 45 is a dangerous game.

I decided that my career and finances had to take a back seat to my well-being. I chose to put myself and my health first. I tied my laces, stepped out the door, and started my running journey all over again.

## Rebuilding Consistency: The Mental Battle of Showing Up

The first two months were a constant battle, both mentally and physically. Even with my background of half-marathons and fast 5Ks, returning after a long break was humbling. There were days when even a light 20-minute jog felt like a mountain to climb. The hardest part wasn't the pace; it was the discipline of showing up three times a week, every single week.

To keep myself accountable and avoid the "embarrassment" of a poor performance, I signed up for a 5K race just one month into my comeback. That looming deadline was exactly the spark I needed. By the end of the second month, I started incorporating strides into my routine. My long runs stretched to 60 minutes, while my other two weekly sessions settled into 40-minute easy runs followed by speed bursts.

In the ninth week, it was time for the race. My body proved that it hadn't forgotten its old habits. I finished the 5K in 23:40—a result that gave me immense confidence. Closing those first nine weeks with a total of 150 kilometers was more than just a good start; it was proof that the system was working.

## From a chore back to a passion

The next three months were all about building the foundation. Running finally shifted from a chore back to a passion. The habit was locked in. Rejoining my old running team made a massive difference; the miles felt lighter, and the long runs became something to look forward to. There’s a specific kind of magic when you’re deep in conversation with teammates and suddenly realize you’ve already hit the 10km mark without even checking your watch.

My long runs gradually stretched to 80 minutes, while my mid-week sessions settled between 40 and 60 minutes. Towards the end of the fifth month, I started introducing "sub-threshold" work—10 to 20-minute segments designed to teach my body how to clear lactate efficiently. The goal was patience: building the engine without redlining it too early.

By the end of month five, it was time for another progress check. I ran a controlled 5K, keeping my average heart rate at 161 bpm. Crossing the line at 22:20 confirmed exactly what I felt: the system was working, and I was moving steadily in the right direction.

## Running smarter, not just more

My plan for the coming months is all about refined progression. Instead of a massive jump in mileage, I’m opting for a steady increase from 30km to 35km per week. To support this growth without risking injury, I’m integrating a dedicated hour of cross-training on the bike, along with two focused 30-minute strength sessions each week.

The strategy is a balanced mix: more sub-threshold running to improve efficiency, cross-training for aerobic capacity, and strength work for durability. This systematic approach should lead me to a 21:15 5K within the next two months. I’m not just running more; I’m running smarter, building the resilience needed for the long road to December 2027.
